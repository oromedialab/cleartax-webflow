01 · Foundations

What is e-invoicing in the Philippines?

Electronic invoicing is the digital issuance, transmission, and storage of sales invoices and official receipts in a structured machine-readable format — submitted to the Bureau of Internal Revenue's Electronic Invoicing System (EIS) in near real-time.

Until now, Philippine businesses relied on printed receipts and manual books of accounts, with sales reporting consolidated into monthly or quarterly returns. RR 11-2025 closes that era — transforming invoicing from a back-office paper exercise into a live, validated, cryptographically-signed data stream to the BIR.

Under the new regulation, e-invoicing operates on two parallel tracks. Track A governs invoice issuance: every invoice must be a structured JSON payload, signed with a JWS (RS256) digital signature, and issued from BIR-registered software holding a valid Acknowledgement Certificate. Track B governs reporting: that same signed payload must reach the BIR's Electronic Invoicing System (EIS) within three calendar days of issuance.

For the buyer, the invoice is still presented as a human-readable document (PDF rendering, email attachment, or printout). For the BIR, every line item, every TIN, and every VAT computation is now visible at the point of sale — and cryptographically tamper-evident.

E-invoicing is not just digitization. It's the convergence of sales recognition, tax computation, and government reporting into a single, automatic event.
02 · The Mandate

The BIR e-invoicing mandate is here. It's bigger than your IT team thinks.

Revenue Regulation 11-2025 is not a form update. It's a fundamental change to how Philippine businesses issue, sign, and report invoices — and the deadline is December 31, 2026.

What you must comply with

Invoice Issuance (Track A). Every invoice must be a structured JSON file, digitally signed with JWS/RS256. PDFs don't qualify. Plain email doesn't qualify. And it must come from BIR-registered software with an active Acknowledgement Certificate (AC).

EIS Reporting (Track B). The same invoice payload must be transmitted to BIR's Electronic Invoicing System within 3 calendar days. Miss the window, and you're non-compliant — even if the invoice itself was perfectly formatted.

Who's in scope. Large Taxpayers, e-commerce businesses, and any enterprise running ERP or accounting software — regardless of revenue. If any branch is in scope, all locations must comply.

The stakes. Non-compliant invoices are invalidated. Input tax deductions are lost. BIR publicly lists non-compliant companies. And failure to issue, transmit, and maintain books are treated as separate offences — one slip can trigger all three.

03 · The System

Understanding the BIR EIS

The Electronic Invoicing System (eis.bir.gov.ph) is the BIR's central platform for receiving, validating, and archiving e-invoice data from covered taxpayers. It is the single source of truth for all electronically transmitted sales records.

EIS operates on a push model: covered taxpayers — or their accredited Sales Data Transmission Systems — push invoice JSON payloads to EIS endpoints. The system validates each submission against the official JSON schema, returns an acknowledgment with a unique reference number, and stores the record in the BIR's data warehouse.

Three operational components sit inside EIS:

The Taxpayer Portal — a web-based interface where taxpayers can manually upload JSON files, view submission history, download acknowledgments, and reconcile transmitted data.

The EIS API — the programmatic endpoint that accredited systems use for real-time, automated transmission. This is the only viable path for businesses with meaningful invoice volumes.

The Sales Data Transmission System (SDTS) accreditation framework — the BIR's certification process for software providers, ERPs, and POS systems that wish to push data to EIS on behalf of their users.

⚡ Critical to know

EIS submission is not optional reporting

Failure to transmit a single covered invoice within the three-calendar-day window is treated as a discrete violation under the NIRC. Each invoice missed is a separately penalized offense — meaning a covered taxpayer issuing 500 invoices a month is potentially exposed to 500 distinct violations for any month of non-compliance.

04 · Scope

Who's in scope under RR 11-2025?

Coverage applies regardless of revenue size — and if any branch is in scope, all locations must comply.

SEGMENT 01

Large Taxpayers

All taxpayers classified under the BIR's Large Taxpayer Service. Mandatory under Wave 1 of RR 11-2025 with the December 31, 2026 deadline.

SEGMENT 02

E-commerce businesses

Online sellers, digital platforms, marketplaces, SaaS providers, and any business earning revenue through electronic transactions — included in Wave 1.

SEGMENT 03

ERP & accounting software users

Any enterprise running ERP, accounting, or CAS software — regardless of revenue size. The presence of the system itself triggers coverage.

SEGMENT 04

Multi-branch enterprises

If any single branch is in scope, all locations of that enterprise must comply. There is no partial rollout permitted within a single TIN.

SEGMENT 05

Exporters & incentive-based (Wave 2)

PEZA-registered enterprises, BOI-registered businesses, and other incentive-based taxpayers come under Wave 2 with the ESRS live reporting regulation.

SEGMENT 06

Voluntary early adopters

Any taxpayer may opt in voluntarily. Early adopters are entitled to 50–100% tax deductions on implementation costs under RR 11-2025 incentives.

05 · The Roadmap

Compliance timeline & rollout phases

From pilot to full-economy rollout — the BIR's phased enforcement approach.

2018 — Foundation

TRAIN Law effectivity

RA 10963 takes effect, introducing Section 237-A and the legal basis for mandatory e-invoicing.

July 2022 — Pilot launch

Top 100 taxpayer EIS pilot

BIR rolls out EIS to the initial cohort of 100 large taxpayers — primarily exporters and e-commerce operators — under RR 8-2022 and RR 9-2022.

2025 — New framework

RR 11-2025 issued

BIR publishes the definitive e-invoicing regulation introducing Track A (JSON + JWS issuance), Track B (3-day EIS reporting), and the Acknowledgement Certificate framework.

31 December 2026 — Wave 1 deadline

Large Taxpayers, e-commerce & ERP users go live

All Wave 1 covered taxpayers must issue RR 11-2025 compliant e-invoices. Non-compliant invoices are invalidated and lose input VAT eligibility from this date forward.

2027 onwards — Wave 2

Exporters & incentive-based businesses

PEZA, BOI, and other incentive-based enterprises come under Wave 2 alongside ESRS live reporting under a separate regulation.

Beyond — Universal coverage

Full-economy rollout

BIR's stated direction is the eventual extension of e-invoicing to substantially all VAT-registered taxpayers, mirroring trajectories already seen in Saudi Arabia, India, and Malaysia.

06 · The Mechanics

How RR 11-2025 compliance actually works

Four sequential events transform a transaction into a fully BIR-compliant e-invoice — across Track A (issuance) and Track B (reporting).

01

Invoice generation

Your ERP creates the sale record. ClearTax pulls the data and assembles all BIR-mandated fields — TIN, document type, line items, VAT, withholding.

02

JSON + JWS signing

The invoice is converted into BIR-compliant JSON and signed with a JWS digital signature using RS256 — the only signature format accepted under RR 11-2025.

03

Buyer delivery (Track A)

The signed JSON is delivered directly to your buyer. No BIR clearance gate to wait on. Human-readable rendering provided alongside the structured payload.

04

EIS transmission (Track B)

The same signed payload is pushed to BIR EIS within 3 calendar days. Idempotent retries, cancellation, and amendment flows — handled automatically.

07 · The Technical Layer

JSON + JWS: the RR 11-2025 spec

RR 11-2025 is explicit about format: structured JSON, signed with JWS/RS256. PDFs don't qualify. Plain email doesn't qualify. Every field is validated server-side.

The BIR JSON schema is organized around three hierarchical objects: the header (issuer TIN, RDO code, document type, control numbers), the parties (seller and buyer information including business style, address, and VAT registration status), and the line items (description, quantity, unit, unit price, line discount, line VAT, line total).

A separate summary object captures totals: gross sales, less VAT-exempt sales, less zero-rated sales, taxable sales, VAT amount, withholding, less other deductions, and net amount due. Fields are typed strictly — currency values must follow the prescribed decimal precision, TINs follow the XXX-XXX-XXX-XXX format, and timestamps use ISO 8601 in Philippine Standard Time.

The JWS signature requirement is non-negotiable. Every payload must be signed using JWS with the RS256 algorithm, bound to a private key whose corresponding public certificate is registered with the BIR through your Acknowledgement Certificate. The signature binds the document to your identity and the moment of issuance — making post-hoc alteration cryptographically detectable.

Common rejection reasons we see at ClearTax: missing buyer TIN on B2B transactions exceeding ₱1,000; mismatched VAT computation between line items and summary; non-sequential or duplicate invoice control numbers; expired or unregistered signing certificates; and incorrect document type codes (sales invoice vs. official receipt vs. credit/debit memo).

08 · The Exposure

The stakes for non-compliance

Under RR 11-2025, failure to issue, transmit, and maintain books are treated as separate offences — one slip can trigger all three.

RR 11-2025 · Enforcement consequences

What's at stake for each violation

  • Invoice invalidation. Non-compliant invoices are invalidated outright — they have no legal standing as tax documents.
  • Lost input VAT deductions. Buyers receiving non-compliant invoices cannot claim input tax credits on those transactions.
  • Public non-compliance lists. BIR publishes lists of non-compliant companies — your name appears alongside repeat offenders.
  • Acknowledgement Certificate revocation. Your AC can be revoked on audit, forcing a full re-application that restarts the compliance clock.
  • Stacked offences. Failure to issue, transmit, and maintain books are separate violations — one transaction can trigger three penalties.
  • Statutory fines & criminal exposure. NIRC §264 and §275 apply: administrative fines, possible imprisonment for willful violations, and suspension under Oplan Kandado.
